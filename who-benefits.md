# WalkFit — Who benefits? (expanded)

This note expands **1.1 Business Objective Statement → Who Benefits** from `walkfit.md`: *who* gains value, *how*, and *what* they might measure—so you can paste sections into a report or defend the stakeholder map in class.

---

## 1. Primary users (end players)

These are the people who install and use WalkFit day to day.

| Segment | What they want | How WalkFit helps |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Weight-management seekers** | Sustainable calorie burn, habit change without extreme diets | Low-friction activity (walking), visible progress (steps, streaks), small wins that stack over weeks |
| **Generally inactive adults** | “I know I should move more” but no routine | Notifications as gentle prompts, game framing (World Trekker) so walking feels like a quest, not a lecture |
| **Beginners / gym-averse** | Avoid intimidation, cost, or complexity | No equipment required at MVP; phone-only path; progression that starts small (achiever path) |
| **Socially motivated users** | Accountability without toxic competition | Optional teams, cheers, cooperative weekly challenges; leaderboards opt-in per `walkfit.md` ethics |

**Value in one line:** WalkFit is built for people who *intend* to be healthier but need **structure, feedback, and optional social proof** to turn walking from an occasional idea into a **repeatable habit**.

---

## 2. Secondary: organizations (if you sell or partner)

WalkFit does not have to stay “consumer-only.” The same mechanics map cleanly to **B2B2C** or **partnership** stories for your assignment.

### Health insurers & payers

- **Why they care:** Inactivity contributes to preventable utilization (e.g. metabolic risk, cardiovascular risk). Even modest increases in regular walking can support population-level wellness narratives.
- **What they might want:** Aggregated engagement metrics (opt-in), program completion, not raw clinical claims—**privacy and consent** are non-negotiable.
- **How WalkFit fits:** A **low-cost digital nudge** layered on top of existing wellness portals; gamification as **adherence**, not treatment.

### Employers & corporate wellness

- **Why they care:** Absenteeism, presenteeism, and benefits cost; HR teams want **participation** in voluntary wellness programs.
- **What they might want:** Team challenges branded to the company, optional leaderboards by department, **no mandatory public ranking** (aligns with your opt-in social design).
- **How WalkFit fits:** “Step challenge month” with cooperative goals (socializer persona) plus individual streaks (achiever persona)—**culture-building** without requiring athleticism.

### Community groups (optional angle)

Clubs, alumni networks, or local running/walking groups could host **private leagues**. Same product pattern as corporate, smaller scale—useful if your proposal needs a **non-corporate** stakeholder example.

---

## 3. Indirect beneficiaries

- **Households / family members:** More activity and routine can support shared goals (e.g. parents modeling behavior)—mention lightly unless your brief asks for family design.
- **Clinicians (high-level):** They rarely “use” the app; they may **recommend** walking. WalkFit is **not** a medical device in this proposal; keep language as **wellness support**, not diagnosis or treatment.

---

## 4. Mapping back to the 6D template

For **Define business objectives**, you can state:

1. **Primary beneficiary:** the individual user (habit + engagement).
2. **Secondary beneficiaries (optional revenue or pilots):** insurers or employers who care about **scale, participation, and cost-effective nudges**.

If your course wants a single sentence for the slide deck:

> **Who benefits:** Everyday users who need help sticking with walking; optionally, organizations that want better participation in wellness programs without expensive hardware.

---

## 5. Risks to name when you expand “Who Benefits”

- **Surveillance optics:** Employers or insurers must not feel like “Big Brother”; emphasize **voluntary enrollment** and **aggregated** reporting.
- **Equity:** Not everyone has equal safe walkable space; your ethics section (rest days, opt-in competition) supports a fair story—reference `walkfit.md` **1.3 Ethics & Risk**.
